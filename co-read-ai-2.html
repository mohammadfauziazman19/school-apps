The Sound of Silence
A narrative by Priya Nair · Adapted for classroom use

Maya stood at the edge of the crumbling pier, her fingers tracing the grooves left by decades of salt and neglect. The harbour below was deathly still, as if holding its breath—waiting for something she could not name. Behind her, the town hummed with the indifferent noise of morning routines: the clatter of shutters, the low groan of delivery trucks, laughter that belonged to someone else's story. She had returned after seven years, and yet nothing about this place seemed to remember her at all.

🔒 Paragraph 2 — Locked
She had told herself the visit was practical—her mother's house needed to be sold, papers signed, a life neatly packaged and handed over to strangers. But standing here, she understood it was something else. The pier was the last place she had felt real, before ambition had made her hollow and efficient. Her therapist called it dissociation. Maya called it survival.
🔒 Paragraph 3 — Locked
A boy, no older than ten, appeared beside her without warning. He did not look at her, only at the water. "You're standing on the soft part," he said. "Everyone does. They think it's the edge that matters." Maya blinked. She moved instinctively, and the boy finally looked up—his expression neither curious nor kind, simply observant, the way old things watch the world.
🔒 Paragraph 4 — Locked
"Did you grow up here?" she asked, because she did not know what else to say to a child who spoke like a tide chart. He shrugged. "My grandfather did. I just visit." He picked up a stone—smooth, grey, unremarkable—and held it out to her. "He said the sea gives back what it's ready to." Maya took the stone. It was heavier than it looked.
🔒 Paragraph 5 — Locked
She watched him walk away, his footsteps confident on the rotting boards. In her palm, the stone felt like a decision she hadn't made yet. The harbour hadn't changed. The town hadn't changed. Perhaps, she thought, she was the one who had been waiting—not for this place to remember her, but to remember herself. She placed the stone in her pocket and turned, for the first time, toward the town.
